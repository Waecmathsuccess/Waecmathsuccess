What is Breast Cancer?

Breast cancer occurs when cells in the breast grow out of control. These cells usually form a tumor that can often be seen on an x-ray or felt as a lump. The tumor is malignant (cancerous) if the cells can grow into surrounding tissues or spread to distant areas of the body.

Early Detection

Early detection of breast cancer is crucial for successful treatment. Regular screenings, including mammograms and clinical breast exams, can help detect cancer in its early stages when it's most treatable. Women aged 40 and older should have mammograms every 1-2 years.

Common Signs and Symptoms

  • New lump in the breast or underarm
  • Thickening or swelling of part of the breast
  • Irritation or dimpling of breast skin
  • Redness or flaky skin in the nipple area
  • Pulling in of the nipple
  • Nipple discharge other than breast milk
  • Any change in the size or shape of the breast
  • Pain in any area of the breast

Risk Reduction

While some risk factors cannot be changed, lifestyle choices can help reduce breast cancer risk:

  • Maintain a healthy weight
  • Exercise regularly
  • Limit alcohol consumption
  • Eat a balanced diet
  • Breastfeed if possible

Screening Guidelines

Regular screening is key to early detection. Recommended screenings include:

  • Monthly breast self-exams
  • Clinical breast exams every 3 years (ages 20-39)
  • Annual mammograms (ages 40+)
  • Additional screening for high-risk individuals

Myths about Breast Cancer

  • Myth: Only women get breast cancer.
    Fact: Men can also develop breast cancer.
  • Myth: Wearing a bra causes breast cancer.
    Fact: There is no scientific evidence linking bras to breast cancer.
  • Myth: Only women with a family history get breast cancer.
    Fact: Most women diagnosed have no family history.
  • Myth: Breast cancer is a death sentence.
    Fact: With early detection, many women survive breast cancer.

Did You Know?

  • Breast cancer is the most common cancer among women worldwide.
  • Approximately 1 in 8 women will develop breast cancer in their lifetime.
  • There are over 3.8 million breast cancer survivors in the United States.
  • Breast cancer can occur in men, though it is rare.

Mind-Blowing Facts

  • The first known case of breast cancer was recorded in ancient Egypt in 1600 B.C.
  • Breast cancer research has significantly improved detection and treatment options over the past few decades.
  • Genetic mutations, such as BRCA1 and BRCA2, can increase the risk of breast cancer.
  • Breastfeeding may lower the risk of breast cancer for some women.
